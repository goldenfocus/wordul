BASTE

verb · 1 syllable · /beɪst/

BASTE

What does "BASTE" mean?

To moisten roasting food with fat or juices, or to stitch fabric loosely as a guide.

Meanings

  1. To spoon or brush juices, fat, or sauce over food as it cooks, to keep it moist. Baste the turkey every twenty minutes so it stays juicy.
  2. To sew with long, loose, temporary stitches that hold pieces in place before final stitching. She basted the hem before running it through the machine. technical
  3. To beat or thrash someone soundly (archaic). The old play has one rogue baste another with a stick. archaic

Word origin

The cooking sense is of uncertain origin, possibly from Old French 'basser' (to moisten); the sewing sense comes separately from Old French 'bastir' (to build, tack together), of Germanic origin.

Remember it

BASTE the roast and BASTE the seam: in both, you're a temporary helper - keeping it moist, or holding it together until the real stitch comes.

What it teaches

Whether on a roast or a seam, basting is the same art: a loose, attentive hold that keeps the thing from drying out or falling apart before it's done.
