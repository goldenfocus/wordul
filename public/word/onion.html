ONION

noun · 2 syllables · /'ʌn.jən/

ONION

What does "ONION" mean?

A round edible bulb with a pungent taste and concentric layers, used widely in cooking.

Meanings

  1. The layered, strong-smelling bulb of a plant in the allium family, eaten raw or cooked as a vegetable and flavouring. He chopped an onion and his eyes began to water within seconds.
  2. Something understood as having many layers, especially when peeling them back reveals more, as in 'layers of an onion'. The mystery was an onion: every answer revealed another question beneath it. figurative

Did you know?

  • Onions make you cry by chemistry, not malice: slicing one releases a sulfur gas that drifts up and reacts with the water in your eyes to form a weak acid, which your tears rush to wash away.
  • The Latin word behind 'onion', 'unio', also meant a single large pearl - so the humble cooking bulb shares a root with the idea of a precious, unified whole.

Word origin

From Latin 'unio', a word for a single large pearl and also a single-bulb onion, via Old French 'oignon'; the sense of 'oneness' captured the bulb's many layers fused into one.

Remember it

ONION begins with 'ONE' (almost) - fitting, since its name comes from Latin 'unio', meaning one unified bulb.
