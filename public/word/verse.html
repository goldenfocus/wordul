VERSE

noun · 1 syllable · /vɜːrs/

VERSE

What does "VERSE" mean?

Writing arranged with a metrical rhythm, typically with a rhyme; a single line or stanza of poetry.

Meanings

  1. Poetry as a form, especially metrical writing as distinct from prose. He wrote the dedication in verse.
  2. A group of lines forming a unit in a poem or song; a stanza. The second verse repeats the opening melody.
  3. A numbered subdivision of a chapter in a religious text such as the Bible. She quoted a verse from the Psalms.

Did you know?

  • A 'verse' was originally a furrow, not a poem: Latin 'versus' named the turn a ploughman makes at a field's end, and a line of poetry borrowed the image - the eye turning back like the plough.

Word origin

From Latin 'versus' meaning 'a turning of the plough, a furrow, a line of writing', from 'vertere' ('to turn') - because writing a line of verse was likened to the turn a ploughman makes at the end of a furrow.

Remember it

VERSE shares 'vers-' (to turn) with 'reVERSE' and 'VERSus' - each line of verse turns the eye back to start the next.

A little poem

The ploughman turns his blade at the field's end;
the poet turns the line, and starts again.

couplet

Wordplay

  • The farmer became a poet naturally - he'd been turning verses in his field for years.

What it teaches

Every good line, like every furrow, is made by knowing exactly where to turn back.
