GUMBO

noun · 2 syllables · /ˈɡʌm.boʊ/

GUMBO

What does "GUMBO" mean?

A thick, hearty Louisiana stew of meat or shellfish, vegetables, and a dark roux, served over rice.

Meanings

  1. A rich stew characteristic of Louisiana Creole and Cajun cooking, thickened with roux, okra, or filé powder. She let the gumbo simmer all afternoon until the roux turned the color of chocolate.
  2. The okra plant or its edible pods, the original sense from which the dish takes its name. The recipe calls for a pound of fresh gumbo sliced thin. archaic
  3. A heavy, sticky clay soil that becomes slick when wet. After the rain the dirt road turned to gumbo and the truck bogged down. informal

Did you know?

  • In 2004 Louisiana made gumbo its official state cuisine, one of the few U.S. states to legislate a signature dish into law.

Word origin

From a West African (Bantu) word for okra, such as Kimbundu 'ki-ngombo'; the term traveled through Louisiana French to name first the vegetable, then the stew thickened with it.

Remember it

GUMBO has okra's gum (its slippery thickening goo) right at the start: G-U-M.
