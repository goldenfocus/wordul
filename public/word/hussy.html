HUSSY

noun · 2 syllables · /'hʌs.i/

HUSSY

What does "HUSSY" mean?

An old-fashioned, often insulting term for a woman regarded as immoral or impudent.

Meanings

  1. A bold, disreputable, or sexually improper woman (now dated and derogatory). In the Victorian novel she is dismissed by neighbours as a brazen hussy. archaic
  2. A cheeky or mischievous girl or woman, said teasingly. "You little hussy," her aunt laughed, swatting at her with a tea towel. informal

Did you know?

  • 'Hussy' is just 'housewife' worn down by centuries of speech - the same Middle English word 'huswif' split in two, one branch staying respectable and the other sliding into an insult, a classic example of how a word's meaning can curdle.

Word origin

A worn-down form of 'housewife': Middle English 'huswif' was pronounced 'hussif' and then narrowed and soured over the centuries into an insult, a textbook case of a respectable word decaying in meaning.

Remember it

HUSSY is HOUSEWIFE with the house knocked out and the meaning soured - same word, sunk reputation.

Wordplay

  • Call her a hussy and you're really just calling her a housewife with the syllables filed off - history did the slander, not you.

What it teaches

Words can sink in status while their owners stay the same - language remembers our prejudices long after we forget them.
